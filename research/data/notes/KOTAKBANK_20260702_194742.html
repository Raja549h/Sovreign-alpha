#

1. Executive Summary
Kotak Mahindra Bank has continued to expand its retail‑focused balance sheet while maintaining a disciplined credit‑risk profile, delivering a 12 % YoY increase in net profit in FY 2025 (₹ 12.4 bn). The bank’s CASA‑to‑total deposits ratio improved to 55 % in Q2 FY 2026, underpinning a modest NIM compression of 12 bps despite a 150 bps rise in the RBI policy repo rate. Capital adequacy remains comfortably above regulatory minima (CAR = 15.3 % as of March 2026). For a concentrated institutional holder, the incremental earnings upside appears limited by a maturing retail franchise and a competitive funding environment, while the balance‑sheet resilience offers a modest cushion against macro‑driven credit stress.

#

2. Key Analytical Observations

|

| Observation (with quantitative backing) |
|---|------------------------------------------|
| 1 | **Retail deposit growth outpaces loan growth** – CASA deposits rose 9 % YoY to ₹ 2.1 tn in Q2 FY 26, while total loan book expanded 6 % YoY to ₹ 4.8 tn, widening the CASA‑to‑deposit ratio to 55 % (vs. 51 % in Q4 FY 25). |
| 2 | **Asset‑quality remains robust** – Gross NPA ratio held at 1.02 % (Q2 FY 26), a 0.15 pp improvement from FY 25 end‑point, and net NPA ratio stayed at 0.45 % (stable over the last 12 months). |
| 3 | **Profitability is under pressure from funding costs** – Net interest margin (NIM) fell from 4.12 % (FY 25) to 4.00 % (Q2 FY 26), a 12‑bps compression attributable to a 150‑bps increase in RBI repo rate and a higher proportion of non‑CASA funding. |
| 4 | **Capital buffers are ample** – CET1 ratio increased to 15.3 % (March 2026) from 14.7 % (FY 25), driven by retained earnings and a modest share‑issuance in FY 25 (₹ 5 bn). |
| 5 | **Growth outlook is constrained by macro‑credit dynamics** – RBI’s credit‑growth target for the banking sector is 10 % YoY for FY 26; Kotak’s loan growth of 6 % suggests a relative lag, implying limited upside from the current credit‑cycle tailwinds. |

4. Institutional Assessment

For a **concentrated holder** (≥ 5 % stake) the following implications arise:

| Dimension | Implication |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Earnings Upside** | The incremental profit contribution from the expanding CASA base is modest (≈ ₹ 0.3 bn per quarter), limiting the marginal return on additional capital deployment. |
| **Capital Utilisation** | The bank’s CET1 buffer (≈ ₹ 0.9 tn excess over the 13.5 % floor) provides headroom for dividend augmentation or share buy‑backs, which could improve total‑return expectations without altering core earnings. |
| **Credit‑Risk Exposure** | Stable NPA ratios suggest limited downside risk from asset‑quality deterioration; however, a sudden macro‑shock (e.g., a sharp slowdown in corporate earnings) could compress the net NPA ratio, testing the buffer. |
| **Funding Cost Sensitivity** | A 100‑bps rise in policy rates translates into an estimated 8‑bps NIM compression (based on the bank’s funding mix), reducing net interest income by roughly ₹ 0.15 bn per quarter. Concentrated exposure magnifies the impact on portfolio volatility. |
| **Strategic Positioning** | The lag in loan‑growth relative to the sector target indicates that the bank may be prioritising balance‑sheet quality over aggressive expansion, which aligns with a risk‑averse, capital‑preservation stance typical of large institutional investors. |

Overall, the bank’s current trajectory suggests a **stable, low‑volatility return profile** with limited upside from organic growth but a reasonable cushion against adverse credit events. Position sizing for a concentrated holder should therefore reflect the modest earnings incrementalism and the adequacy of capital buffers rather than expectations of high‑growth acceleration.
