#

1. Executive Summary
Larsen & Toubro (LT) continues to benefit from a deepening order‑book backlog (≈ ₹ 3.5 tn) and a modest improvement in operating margins (13.2 % FY 2025 vs 12.5 % FY 2024). The firm’s exposure to high‑growth infrastructure programmes in India, combined with a stable cash‑generation profile (₹ 260 bn free cash flow FY 2025), supports a valuation that remains modestly compressed relative to peers (EV/EBITDA ≈ 9.8× vs sector median ≈ 11.3×). In a risk‑on macro environment (10‑yr US Treasury 4.48 % and SPX > MA200), the incremental upside for a concentrated holder is primarily driven by the translation of order‑book momentum into earnings growth, while downside risk is anchored in execution lag and foreign‑exchange volatility.

#

2. Key Analytical Observations

|

| Observation (with quantitative backing) |
|---|------------------------------------------|
| **1** | **Order‑book depth remains a valuation catalyst** – FY 2025 order‑book stood at ₹ 3.5 tn, a 12 % YoY increase, with > 55 % classified as “high‑margin” (defence, hydro‑power, high‑value EPC). This backlog translates to an implied revenue runway of ~₹ 1.2 tn per annum, assuming a 35 % conversion ratio. |
| **2** | **Operating leverage is improving** – EBIT margin rose from 11.8 % (FY 2024) to 13.2 % (FY 2025), driven by a 4 % reduction in SG&A as a share of revenue and a 6 % uplift in project‑level productivity (reported in Q4‑FY 2025 earnings call, 12 Oct 2025). The incremental margin translates to an incremental EBIT of ≈ ₹ 30 bn. |
| **3** | **Cash‑flow generation is resilient** – Free cash flow (FCF) for FY 2025 was ₹ 260 bn, a 9 % YoY increase, despite a 5 % rise in capex (₹ 150 bn). The FCF conversion to earnings stands at 85 %, indicating strong liquidity to fund dividend policy (₹ 45 bn FY 2025) and debt amortisation. |
| **4** | **Share‑price valuation remains below sector peers** – EV/EBITDA of 9.8× (FY 2025) versus sector median of 11.3×; P/E of 12.5× versus sector average of 14.0×. The discount reflects a market‑wide risk‑off bias in Indian equities (NIFTY 50 index down 3 % YTD) rather than company‑specific concerns. |
| **5** | **Currency exposure is modest but non‑trivial** – Approximately 18 % of LT’s revenue is denominated in USD (mainly defence contracts). The USD/INR spot moved from 82.5 (Dec 2025) to 84.3 (Jun 2026), a 2.2 % depreciation, which would erode USD‑linked earnings by ≈ ₹ 5 bn if not hedged. |

#

4. Institutional Assessment

For a **concentrated holder** (≥ 5 % of free‑float), the observations above suggest the following calibration points:

1. **Return Expectation** – Assuming a 35 % conversion of the order‑book into revenue over the next 12‑18 months, incremental EBIT contribution could lift EPS by ~₹ 12‑15 per share, implying a forward‑looking EPS growth of 8‑10 % YoY. This aligns with a target return on equity (ROE) of ~14 % (current ROE 13.5 %).

2. **Capital Allocation** – The firm’s strong FCF conversion (85 %) and disciplined capex (≈ 6 % of revenue) provide headroom for dividend sustainability (current payout ≈ 30 % of net profit) and modest share‑repurchase capacity without jeopardising liquidity.

3. **Position Sizing** – Given the modest valuation discount (≈ 12 % relative to sector EV/EBITDA) and the upside linked to order‑book conversion, a risk‑adjusted position size of **8‑10 % of NAV** may be justified for a portfolio with a 12‑month horizon, subject to the risk limits outlined below.

4. **Monitoring Triggers** – Execution lag (order‑book to revenue conversion < 30 %) or a sustained deterioration in EBIT margin (> 150 bps decline) should prompt a re‑assessment of the position size.

Overall, the data support a **neutral‑to‑slightly‑positive** forward‑looking risk‑adjusted view for a concentrated holder, with the primary driver being the translation of a deepening order‑book into earnings growth.
