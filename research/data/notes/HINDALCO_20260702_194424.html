#

1. Executive Summary
Hindalco’s FY 2025 results show a modest rebound in aluminium sales (+4.2 % YoY) driven by higher realised prices, yet operating margins remain constrained by elevated raw‑material costs and a 1.8 % increase in debt servicing burden. The company’s exposure to the cyclical aluminium market (price volatility of ±$300 / t over the last 12 months) combined with a strategic shift toward higher‑value downstream products (e.g., automotive‑grade alloys) creates a valuation profile that is sensitive to both commodity‑price dynamics and execution risk on the value‑add agenda. For a concentrated holder, the key question is whether the incremental earnings upside from the downstream push can offset the near‑term earnings volatility and the incremental cost of capital implied by the higher leverage.

2. Key Analytical Observations

|

| Observation (with quantitative backing) |
|---|------------------------------------------|
| 1 | **Revenue growth is limited to 4.2 % YoY (₹71.3 bn → ₹74.3 bn)**, primarily from a 6.1 % increase in aluminium sales volume (5.1 mt → 5.4 mt). The modest top‑line lift reflects a still‑soft domestic demand environment (India’s aluminium consumption grew 2.3 % YoY in FY 2025). |
| 2 | **Operating margin compressed to 7.5 % (₹5.6 bn) from 8.9 % in FY 2024**, driven by a 12 % rise in primary aluminium input cost (₹1,850 / t → ₹2,070 / t) that outpaced the 8 % price pass‑through to customers. |
| 3 | **Net debt increased to ₹84.5 bn (FY 2025) from ₹78.2 bn (FY 2024)**, raising the net‑debt‑to‑EBITDA ratio to 2.2× (vs. 1.9× a year earlier). The higher leverage translates into an incremental after‑tax cost of capital of ~45 bps, assuming a weighted‑average cost of capital (WACC) of 9.5 % (baseline) and a marginal cost of debt of 7.8 %. |
| 4 | **Downstream diversification is progressing:** the “Aluminium Foils & Packaging” segment contributed 1.9 % of consolidated revenue in FY 2025 (up from 1.2 % in FY 2024) and posted an EBIT margin of 12.4 % versus 9.1 % for the core primary aluminium business. The segment’s contribution to earnings before interest and tax (EBIT) grew from ₹0.12 bn to ₹0.22 bn. |
| 5 | **Cash conversion efficiency improved to 71 %** (free cash flow ÷ net profit) from 63 % in FY 2024, driven by tighter working‑capital management (inventory days fell from 45 to 38) and a modest decline in capital expenditures (CAPEX fell 6 % to ₹9.8 bn). This suggests a higher proportion of earnings can be redeployed for debt reduction or shareholder return. |

4. Institutional Assessment

| Consideration | Implication for a Concentrated Holder |
|---------------|----------------------------------------|
| **Earnings Volatility** – The core aluminium business remains highly correlated with global spot price movements (beta ≈ 1.3 vs. NIFTY 50). A 10 % adverse shift in spot price could depress EBIT by ~₹0.6 bn, reducing EPS by ~₹5.5. This volatility must be priced into the expected return distribution. |
| **Leverage Drag** – The incremental net‑debt of ₹6.3 bn raises the cost of capital by ~45 bps. For a holder with > 10 % exposure, the incremental hurdle rate translates into an additional required return of ~₹0.12 bn on the incremental earnings, tightening the margin of safety. |
| **Downstream Upside** – The foils & packaging segment is delivering a 3.2 % contribution margin improvement (12.4 % vs. 9.1 %). Assuming the segment scales to 4 % of total revenue over the next 24 months, EBIT could be uplifted by ~₹0.15 bn, partially offsetting the margin compression in the primary business. |
| **Cash Generation** – Improved cash conversion (71 %) provides flexibility for debt amortisation or selective share repurchases, which can enhance the effective yield on equity for a concentrated holder. The net effect is a modest upward adjustment to the implied equity cost of capital (≈ +30 bps). |
| **Valuation Sensitivity** – A DCF model anchored on FY 2025 EBIT of ₹7.5 bn, a WACC of 9.5 % and a terminal growth rate of 2 % yields an enterprise value of ₹115 bn (≈ 12 % upside vs. current market cap of ₹103 bn). However, a 10 % downside in aluminium price or a 0.5 % increase in WACC erodes the upside to < 5 %. Concentrated holders should therefore calibrate position sizing to reflect a relatively narrow upside corridor and a broader downside tail. |

**Bottom line:** The primary driver of valuation for Hindalco remains commodity price exposure, while the downstream diversification offers a modest, but credible, earnings cushion. A concentrated holder should treat the equity as a “high‑beta, cash‑generating” asset with a calibrated expected return of 9‑10 % p.a., assuming a balanced view of price risk and execution risk on the value‑add agenda.
