#

1. Executive Summary
Maruti Suzuki continues to consolidate its leadership in the Indian passenger‑car market, delivering a 12 % YoY increase in unit sales (1.62 mn units) and a 9 % rise in net profit (₹7.03 bn) in FY 2024. The company’s earnings multiple (P/E ≈ 30×) remains elevated relative to peers, reflecting pricing of near‑term growth rather than a structural shift in valuation. The key institutional implication is that a concentrated holding must weigh the incremental upside from volume expansion against the compression risk embedded in the current multiple, especially under a macro‑environment where the 10‑year yield is 4.48 % (risk‑on) and equity markets are trading above their 200‑day moving average.

4. Institutional Assessment

For a **concentrated holder**, the following considerations are material:

1. **Upside levers** – The 12 % volume growth is primarily driven by the sub‑₹ 5 lakh price‑sensitive segment, where Maruti retains ~55 % market share. Continued share‑gain in this segment can sustain earnings growth without requiring aggressive price hikes, which would be constrained by price‑elastic demand.

2. **Multiple compression risk** – The current EV/EBITDA premium (≈ 26 % above the sector average) is priced on the expectation of persistent volume acceleration. A deceleration to the sector‑average growth rate (≈ 6 % YoY) would necessitate a multiple contraction of ~2‑3 pts to re‑align with peers, implying a potential 8‑10 % decline in market‑cap value.

3. **Cash‑flow buffer** – High operating‑cash‑flow conversion (84 %) and a disciplined dividend policy provide a cushion against short‑term earnings volatility, allowing the fund to maintain a higher position size while preserving downside protection.

4. **Macro‑sensitivity** – The risk‑on regime (10‑yr yield 4.48 %) supports equity valuations, but a shift to a risk‑off stance (e.g., 10‑yr yield >5 %) could pressure consumer financing costs, directly affecting Maruti’s sales velocity. A concentrated exposure should therefore be calibrated with a modest cash reserve to hedge against a potential credit‑cost shock.

Overall, the **valuation implication** for a concentrated holder is that the forward‑looking earnings multiple is the primary driver of expected return. The investor should model scenarios where volume growth reverts to the sector mean and assess the resultant impact on the implied cost of capital and target return.
