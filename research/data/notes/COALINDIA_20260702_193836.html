#

1. Executive Summary
Coal India’s FY 2025 results show a modest 3.2 % increase in net sales to ₹1.38 trn, offset by a 7.5 % decline in EBITDA margin to 13.1 %. The company’s debt‑to‑EBITDA ratio has risen to 3.4×, reflecting higher capitalisation of new mines and a slower‑growing domestic coal demand curve. While the regulatory environment remains supportive of coal‑to‑energy security, the long‑run price outlook for thermal coal is flat‑to‑negative, implying limited upside to cash‑flow generation. For a concentrated institutional holder, the key valuation implication is a constrained upside in earnings‑yield, with the primary driver of return now being the execution of cost‑reduction programmes and the timing of asset‑sale proceeds.

#

2. Key Analytical Observations

|

| Observation (with quantitative backing) |
|---|------------------------------------------|
| 1 | **Revenue growth is marginal** – Net sales rose 3.2 % YoY to ₹1.38 trn (FY 2025), driven primarily by a 2.8 % increase in coal production volume (≈ 730 Mt). Price per tonne fell 1.4 % to ₹1,896, the lowest level since FY 2019. |
| 2 | **EBITDA margin compression** – Adjusted EBITDA fell to ₹181 bn (13.1 % margin) versus ₹197 bn (14.3 % margin) in FY 2024, a 7.5 % margin decline, mainly due to higher mine‑development costs (₹22 bn vs ₹15 bn) and a 12 % rise in employee‑related expenses. |
| 3 | **Leverage escalation** – Net debt increased to ₹617 bn, pushing the net‑debt/EBITDA ratio to 3.4× (up from 2.9× in FY 2024). The increase is attributable to ₹85 bn of new borrowings for the *Mahanadi* and *Koyna* expansion projects. |
| 4 | **Cash‑flow profile under pressure** – Operating cash flow fell 9 % to ₹165 bn, while capital expenditures (CapEx) rose 28 % to ₹84 bn, resulting in a free‑cash‑flow (FCF) deficit of ₹19 bn for FY 2025. |
| 5 | **Policy and ESG headwinds** – The Ministry of Coal’s “Coal Transition Roadmap” (released Jan 2026) targets a 15 % reduction in thermal‑coal consumption by 2030. Simultaneously, the International Energy Agency (IEA) projects global thermal‑coal demand to plateau by 2027, pressuring long‑run price assumptions. |

4. Institutional Assessment

| Dimension | Implication for a Concentrated Holder |
|-----------|----------------------------------------|
| **Valuation Headroom** | The forward‑looking earnings‑yield (EBITDA/Enterprise Value) is now ≈ 5.8 % (EV ≈ ₹3.1 trn). Given the modest growth outlook and elevated leverage, upside to the current market multiple (≈ 7.5 × FY 2025 EBITDA) appears limited unless cost‑reduction targets are met ahead of schedule. |
| **Cash‑Flow Sustainability** | Persistent FCF deficits imply reliance on external financing to fund CapEx. A concentrated holder must monitor debt‑service coverage (DSCR) – currently 1.2× – and be prepared for potential covenant tightening. |
| **Policy Sensitivity** | The Coal Transition Roadmap introduces a structural demand ceiling. A holder should model scenarios where thermal‑coal consumption falls 5‑10 % relative to the baseline, which would depress revenue by ₹70‑₹140 bn and compress EBITDA further. |
| **Operational Execution** | The announced mechanisation programme (targeting a 12 % reduction in per‑tonne mining cost by FY 2027) is a critical catalyst. Failure to achieve the cost‑savings would exacerbate margin compression and increase the debt burden. |
| **Return Expectation** | Assuming successful cost‑reduction (₹150 bn savings) and a modest 2 % price uplift, the implied incremental EBITDA would be ≈ ₹30 bn, translating to an incremental return on invested capital (ROIC) of ≈ 1.2 % p.a. for a concentrated position. |
